Stereotype Threat

Stereotype threat refers to the diminished performance in a situation that would confirm a negative stereotype about one's group. For example, women given the same standardized math test did worse when their gender was emphasized, and they were made more aware of the gender stereotype that women don't do as well as men in math. Their performance was undermined by the concern that they would confirm this negative stereotype about women.

The factors involved in stereotype threat involve undermining the efficiency of working memory, including increased anxiety and negative thoughts which can act to reduce working memory capacity, as well as lowered performance expectations. For example, when under stereotype threat, the activity can seem overwhelming given self perception of abilities. Lower performance expectations can also lead to reduced effort. The anxiety of battling the negative stereotypes about one's group reduces the attention one has for the task at hand, and can decrease performance.

Most of the research on stereotype threat has been in an academic context, examining the effect of stereotype threat on women in math, or minorities in college. One aspect of stereotype threat that is particularly disabling is that people who most value high performance are at greater risk of stereotype threat. Some of the research has focused on mechanisms to aleviate stereotype threat. Allowing people to afirm their self worth by encouraging them to think about skills or values that they think are important is one mechanism. Another is to provide an external attribution for the anxiety or difficulty they experience. For example, telling students that the anxiety they feel is due to an external cause, such as stereotype threat, improves performance on tests.

Translating these research findings to a workplace context is less well studied, but is an important question for fields where women and minorities are under-represented like the tech industry. Application of the research to the workplace suggests that explicitly addressing negative stereotypes is helpful, as is reducing the power of negative stereotypes by focusing on the indentities that transcend race of gender, such as shared values or goals. Role models from under represented groups can also help.
